01 · Main Pillar
Engineering

The Architect's Origin

Since childhood, my answer to "what do you want to be when you grow up?" was unconventional: "I want to be a God." Not out of ego — out of a fascination with autonomous intelligent systems. The moment I brought my first microcontroller to life in undergrad, my mission was set: building intelligent systems that protect people. Today, I lead R&D for commercial vehicle ADAS — designing systems that save lives.

Systems Engineering Philosophy

Since 2009, I have evolved a core belief: I don't just lead teams — I build robust development architectures. My approach is rooted in the V-Model of both software and people, ensuring that scalability is baked into the process from day one. I act as the high-bandwidth bridge between strategic vision and the tactical reality of the engineering floor — and I maintain my technical edge deliberately. I never ask for an output I don't fundamentally understand.

"A leader must remain functional in the field. The moment you stop understanding what your team builds, you stop being an engineer — and start being a passenger."

02 · Pillar
Growth Mindset

Beyond the Theory

You can read books about the Growth Mindset and believe in the theory, but you cannot advocate for it until you have been tested. To me, it isn't a soft skill — it is a calculated risk adventure. I allow myself and the people around me to make mistakes, but only once. A mistake is a data point. A repetition is a system failure.

Curiosity as a Competitive Advantage

If I have one talent, it is curiosity. I leverage it to make difficult decisions that others avoid. Every high-stakes pivot in my life was rewarded — though often preceded by significant work and suffering. That is the price of the adventure.

The Six Pivots

  • The Pivot — left a secure job for a post-grad degree
  • The Reset — moved to Germany with zero language skills
  • The Crisis — started career in 2008 at below-average wage to enter the right field
  • The Gamble — invested every dime in real estate to learn the mechanics firsthand
  • The Failure — started a drop-shipping company that failed but taught me Business 101
  • The Grind — sat the CFA in my 40s, balancing fatherhood and an ADAS career
I am curious. I learn. I fail. I learn even more. Then, and only then, I grow. Growth is a system — and like every system, it must be examined continuously.
03 · Pillar
Finance

From Saving to Systems

I grew up in a classic Egyptian middle-class household. My father, a chemical engineer, taught me that security comes from a well-paying job and disciplined saving. I have a visceral hatred for waste — whether it is inefficient code or unnecessary luxury. But I realized that saving alone doesn't scale wealth. It only preserves it.

The Analytical Deep-Dive

To understand the difference between rich and middle-class, I did what any engineer would do: I reverse-engineered the system. I didn't just read books — I took an unpaid internship at a real estate firm, renovated homes with my own hands to understand the hardware of property, and eventually acquired multiple investment objects in Germany.

CFA Level 1 — First Attempt

I codified this knowledge by challenging the CFA Level 1 exam and passing on the first attempt. This isn't just a certificate — it's a commitment to professional-grade analytical rigour. I now view every engineering R&D roadmap through the Investment Triangle:

  • Risk — what can go wrong, and what's the exposure?
  • Return — what is the measurable upside?
  • Time-to-Value — when does this compound?
"In the lab, money is not for spending — it is a resource to be allocated for maximum system growth."
04 · Pillar
Fitness & Nutrition

The Restless Engine

My mother always described me as "restless." I had a biological need to move — always. I started sports at age six and haven't stopped since. After five years of Karate and reaching a brown belt, I hit a wall: a three-year wait for the black belt. Being too restless to wait, I pivoted to Handball at age 11. That is where my real training began.

The Five-Year Breakthrough

I'll be honest: I was the talentless player. Fit enough to make the team, making enough mistakes to be the target of frustration. I spent five years training relentlessly — gym sessions, basketball, tennis, volleyball, Judo, Kung Fu — all to find the missing edge. One day, the work paid off. I became a key high-scorer for the U18s and was scouted for the First Team.

Consistency eventually overcomes a lack of initial calibration. I have tested this thesis across every domain of my life — and it has never failed.

Certified Optimization

After moving to Germany, I decided to codify this experience. I became a Certified Fitness & Nutrition Coach specializing in Functional Training. Today, I apply the same high-performance logic to individuals and teams that I apply to my engineering projects.
